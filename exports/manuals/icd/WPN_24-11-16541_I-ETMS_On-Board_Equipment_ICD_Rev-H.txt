1Introduction
1 Introduction
This section defines the purpose and scope, identifies referenced documents, provides definitions for key 
elements and acronyms, and provides an organizational overview of the I-ETMS On-Board Equipment 
Interface Control Document.
1.1 Purpose
The purpose of this Interface Control Document (ICD) is to describe the interfaces of the I-ETMS On-Board 
system to a locomotive, cab car, or Multiple Unit that will be equipped.
1.2 Scope
This document describes the I-ETMS On-Board system hardware.  It does not describe system behavior, 
interfaces to other I-ETMS segments, such as the Office Segment or Wayside Segment, or use and 
operation of the system.
1.3 References
The following document is referenced by this user guide:
 l I-ETMS Air Brake Interface Requirements White Paper, WCR-TWP-1008.
 l ATCS Specification 110: Environmental Requirements.
1.4 Definitions
Term Description
I-ETMS A Positive Train Control on-board system that performs train control functions based on 
Global Positioning Information (GPS), surveyed track data, and information from the 
back office and waysides. The I-ETMS On-Board System determines locomotive 
speed, location, and direction of travel in order to navigate, predict, and enforce all 
operational rules and regulations
PTC A technology that prevents train-to-train collisions, derailments caused by excessive 
speed, unauthorized incursions into established work zones, and train movement 
through a track switch left in the wrong position.

2Overview
2 Overview
The Wabtec Railway Electronics I-ETMS®  (Interoperable Electronic Train Management System) is a 
Positive Train Control (PTC) system that provides significant safety and productivity benefits to the railroad. 
This modular system includes a Train Management Computer (TMC), Cab Display Unit (CDU), 
Communications System, and other ancillary components. Software running on the train control system is 
used to perform all train control functions such as determine current position, calculate braking distance, 
manage restrictions, manage off-board communications, and communicate with the operator display.
Primary Function of I-ETMS System
The primary function of I-ETMS is to enforce a train's authority limits, enforce speed limits, provide protection 
within work zones, and control train movement through misaligned main track switches. I-ETMS is a 
locomotive-centric, vital overlay train control system that works in conjunction with existing methods of 
operation to improve the level of railroad safety and protect against human error.
2.1 Certification
Installation, operation, and maintenance of Positive Train Control (PTC) systems, such as Wabtec’s 
I-ETMS® system, are regulated by Federal Railroad Administration rules, specifically Part 236 Subpart I (49 
CFR 236).  Those rules require Certification of PTC systems by the FRA.
It is the responsibility of the railroad to obtain PTC system certification from the FRA, and comply with all 
FRA regulations governing PTC systems.
The process by which a railroad achieves PTC System Certification requires submission of a PTC Safety 
Plan (PTCSP) by the railroad.
The PTCSP is required to include “the training, qualification, and designation program for workers regardless 
of whether those railroad employees will perform inspection, testing, and maintenance tasks involving the 
PTC system.” Further, the PTCSP must also “identify specific procedures and test equipment necessary to 
ensure the safe operation, installation, repair, modification and testing of the product.”
This Interface Control Document does not include worker training and qualification, nor specific installation 
procedures and test equipment, all of which are required for PTC System Certification.
2.2 Optional Features
I-ETMS systems may optionally be configured to detect “Train Handling Exceptions” and interface to “Energy 
Management” software applications. Optimal performance of these features requires certain signals be 
interfaced to the system. There is more detail about these signals described in section 4.2, 'Connections to 
Other Systems.’
A description of these functions is beyond the scope of this document, and may be found in other Wabtec 
I-ETMS product descriptions.

3.8 Propulsion Monitoring Hardware
Pulling force of the locomotive, represented as either Tractive Effort or through Traction Motor Current and 
Generator Voltage, aid I-ETMS in predicting locomotive speed. These signals are not absolutely required for 
I-ETMS, but better system performance is obtained when they are available. For the “LIG” configuration, 
these signals are provided through the network connection.
On DC locomotives without a “LIG” configuration both a VM 03 generator voltage sensor and a CM XX 
(determined by shunt type) current module are used to determine pulling force. Each of these modules 
provides an isolated measurement that is presented to the TMC DIO module as a frequency proportional to 
the voltage or current being measured.
On AC locomotives, the TM-02 module is used without a “LIG” configuration that have a +/- 10VDC output to 
indicate tractive effort. The TM-02 converts the low voltage analog signal into an electrically isolated signal 
that is presented to the TMC DIO module as a frequency proportional to tractive effort.

3.11 Cut-Out Switch Assembly
The Cut-Out Switch Assembly interfaces to the “EBI-300” Brake Interface module on the TMC to allow 
bypass of the ETMS system in the event of a system failure. I-ETMS provides outputs to the locomotive 
brake system through a penalty interface and an emergency interface. I-ETMS also provides an output to the 
locomotive horn through a connection to the electro-pneumatic horn valve. A fault in either the penalty or 
emergency circuits of the EBI would cause the train brakes to apply and would require intervention from the 
crew to release them. The purpose of this module is to provide the capacity for that crew interaction. In the 
same manner, a fault in the horn circuit could cause the horn to sound continuously and would require crew 
interaction to remedy the problem.
Since each of these circuits operates independently from one another, and since the I-ETMS system can still 
function in the presence of a failure of only one of these circuits, the Cut-Out Switch Assembly will provide 
independent cut-out control of the penalty, emergency, and horn interfaces.

patterns:

axle during operation. The signal this axle generator creates is very specific to the required input of the DIO

4.3 Brake and Horn Connections
The I-ETMS system provides a safety critical interface to the locomotive penalty and emergency brake 
system as well as the horn through the TMC EBI module.  Isolation of 2000 volts separates the penalty, 
emergency, horn and cab signal functions. The module provides an isolated 32VDC output at a maximum 
current level of 500 mA for operation of locomotive electronic braking circuitry. It will also operate from 
locomotive supplied 74VDC if electronic braking is not used.
The penalty drive circuitry consists of a pair of isolated solid state switches which are used to drive both the 
source and return sides of the penalty circuit. In the event of power loss, circuit failure, or module fault these 
switches go to an open circuit state generating a penalty. The switches have the capability of driving up to 
1.5A continuous current.
Page4-8 Copyright©WabtecCorporation2010-2020. 24-11-16541,Rev.H
Allrightsreserved.

4SystemInterconnect
I-ETMS includes the capability to multiplex an external cab signal 32VDC penalty signal with the I-ETMS 
penalty signal for situations where air brake penalty inputs are limited.  In that configuration, either I-ETMS or 
Cab Signal can drive the penalty output of I-ETMS and each of I-ETMS and Cab Signal have independent 
cutout inputs to suppress a penalty from the corresponding device.
The emergency drive circuit has a normally open solid state switch operating over the range of 24VDC to a 
maximum of 135VDC and is capable of controlling up to 1A of continuous current. This voltage is obtained 
from the locomotive supplied 74VDC.
The horn drive circuit also uses a normally open solid state switch operating over the range of 24VDC to a 
maximum of 135VDC. This circuit is capable of controlling up to 1A continuous current. This voltage is 
obtained from the locomotive supplied 74VDC.
Two discrete input pairs are used to detect the presence of input voltages equal to or greater than 24VDC. 
The absence is indicated by a voltage equal to or less than 7VDC. These discretes are to indicate horn 
actuation or cab signal system cut out.

4.3.2.7 Pneumatic Requirements
A Pneumatic Horn Valve (PHV) has the following requirements:
 l The horn activates due to an operator action (pulling the horn lever or pressing the horn button), or  if

 l The Pneumatic Pressure Switch closes to deactivate the horn, this switch requires a check valve to

 l When signaled by the On-Board System,the magnetic valve (or solenoid) activates to allow air to pass 
through the magnetic valve to the horn. A check valve blocks this air to prevent the pressure switch 
from closing due to On-Board System horn activation.
